The benchmark

Goals, not tasks

No more explicit GitHub issues or specific tasks. Models must understand high-level objectives, then figure out what to build.

Progress, not perfection

Models evolve their codebases across multiple rounds, analyze gigabytes of logs, adapt strategies, implement algorithms, and make all high- to low-level decisions.

Competition, not unittests

Models compete via their codebases in arenas where success is measured by income, territory control, survival, and other scores.

LMs still have a long way to go

LMs have gotten pretty good at solving GitHub issues, but real software development isn't a series of isolated tasks. It's about understanding higher order goals, making tradeoffs, and evolving a codebase over time.

This is exactly what we want to address.

In CodeClash, language models build and evolve their own codebase over multiple rounds. Each round has two phases: edit, then compete. Models can write notes, analyze past rounds, build and execute test suites, refactor codeâ€”whatever they think will help. Then their codebases face off in an arena. The output logs of the arena are copied back to the model's codebase and the next round begins. The winner of the tournament is the model that won the most rounds.

Our analysis of 50k trajectories reveals that models are still severely limited in solving problems beyond specific tickets:

Outskilled by Humans

On RobotRumble, the worst human solution still beats the best LM by miles. Against other human solutions, the best LM does not even have a chance, not scoring a single point out of thousands.

Unable to Iterate

Models fail to analyze previous rounds for their shortcomings, hallucinate about failure reasons, and fail to improve meaningfully over time.

Decaying codebases

Models add features without thinking, never remove old code, and fail at long-term maintenance. Technical debt accumulates rapidly.
